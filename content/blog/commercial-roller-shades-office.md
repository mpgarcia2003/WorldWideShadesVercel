## What makes a roller shade ‘commercial’

Commercial roller shades prioritize durability, safety, and consistent performance across many windows.
Expect heavier tubes, stronger brackets, and fabrics specified for glare control and heat management.
In offices, the goal is usually visual comfort: reducing screen glare while maintaining daylight and outside view.

## Glare control 101: openness factors that work for workspaces

Solar screen fabrics are common in offices and are specified by openness (1%, 3%, 5%, 10%).
For most offices, 3% or 5% is the sweet spot: strong glare reduction while still letting you see out.
If the building faces harsh sun (south/west), 1% can be worth it—especially in conference rooms where presentations matter.

## Sizing large windows: hardware and deflection considerations

Large spans need larger tubes so the fabric stays aligned and doesn’t ‘smile’ in the center.
As widths push past ~96 inches, motorization often becomes more practical than manual chain operation.
For multi-shade walls, consistent bracket heights and precise leveling prevent a visibly uneven line across the space.
Order free fabric samples from /swatches before you commit—color and openness can look different in your light.

## Motorization and controls: what businesses should budget for

A quality motorization upgrade often adds about $250–$650 per shade depending on size and control type.
Centralized controls (wall switches, timed schedules, or smart building integration) can add upfront cost but reduce daily management.
If you’re evaluating ROI, focus on productivity (less glare, fewer complaints) and HVAC load reduction rather than just the hardware cost.

Specify wipeable fabrics for kitchens/break rooms and high-touch areas.
Use secured chain tensioners or cordless/motorized systems to meet safety expectations.
Plan a standard fabric/color across departments to simplify future replacements.

## FAQ


### How do I measure for commercial roller shades for offices and workspaces?

Measure width and height in three places, use the smallest width for inside-mount, and always double-check depth and obstructions like handles or trim.

### Should I choose light filtering or blackout?

Choose light filtering for living spaces where you want daylight and glare control; choose blackout for bedrooms, nurseries, media rooms, or any room where sleep or presentations matter.

### What is a realistic budget per window?

For most custom roller-style shades, plan roughly $120–$420 per window, with motorization adding about $250–$650 when desired.

### Can I order samples first?

Yes—samples are the fastest way to confirm color, texture, and light behavior in your home. Order them from /swatches and test morning vs. afternoon light.

## Pro measurement checklist (print this before you measure)

Write down each window name (for example: ‘Primary Bedroom – Left’) and record width at the top/middle/bottom and height at left/center/right.
Note obstructions: cranks, alarms, trim projections, tile returns, or anything that could interfere with a bracket.
If you’re outside-mounting, sketch where the brackets will land and confirm you have solid material for anchors.
Finally, verify your control side (left/right) based on furniture placement and outlet location if you plan motorization.
