## What makes historic windows different
Most historic windows differ from modern ones in ways that affect shade selection.

### Deeper trim and irregular reveals
Old window frames aren’t always perfectly square.
That matters because roller shades need a level tube to track straight.

### Sensitive finishes
Painted or shellacked trim can scratch easily.
A poor install can chip edges or leave visible holes.

### Taller windows and unique proportions
Victorian and Colonial-era windows are often taller than modern standards.
That can push you toward sturdier tubes and better hardware.

## Mounting strategies that protect trim and reduce light gaps
Mount choice determines both appearance and performance.

### Inside mount: best for a built-in look
Inside mount can look original and integrated, especially when the casing is deep.
But you must account for:
- Side light gaps
- Clearance for hardware
- Out-of-square frames

### Outside mount: best for blocking light and hiding irregularities
Outside mount can cover uneven reveals and reduce side light.
It also lets you avoid drilling into delicate inner jambs.

If you’re trying to approach blackout darkness in a bedroom, read [blackout curtains vs blackout shades](/blog/blackout-curtains-vs-blackout-shades).

### Cassette/headrail for a finished period-friendly top line
A cassette can make the shade look more intentional and can help protect the roll from dust.

## Measuring and ordering for old trim (avoid the most common mistakes)
Historic trim profiles vary, and a single measurement can be misleading.

### Measure width in three places
Take a top, middle, and bottom width measurement.
If they differ, you may be dealing with a slightly out-of-square opening.
In that case, consider:
- Outside mounting to cover irregular edges
- Using the smallest width for inside mount so the shade runs freely

### Measure depth for inside mounts
Deep casings are common in older homes, but obstructions can reduce usable depth:
- Window latches
- Interior storm windows
- Decorative stops

### Decide how much light gap you can tolerate
Inside mounts almost always show side gaps.
If you want the tightest look, combine outside mount with a valance or layered side panels.

## FAQ: Roller shades for historic homes

### Will roller shades look out of place in a Victorian home?
Not if you choose textured fabrics, warm colors, and a clean valance or cassette. The fabric choice matters more than the mechanism.

### Should I drill into original trim?
When possible, choose mounting points that minimize damage. Outside mounts can avoid delicate inner jambs, and careful inside mounts can still look built-in.

### How do I handle out-of-square windows?
Measure at multiple points and consider outside mounting or a slightly larger shade to cover irregular edges.

### Can I use solar shades in a historic home?
Yes. Solar fabrics can protect floors and furniture while preserving a view, especially on sun-exposed windows.

### Are cordless shades recommended for historic homes?
Yes. Cordless keeps the look clean and is safer for families, especially in older homes with tall windows.
