## Average Cost Ranges by Shade Type

These are real-world 2026 prices for custom-made shades (not big-box store specials) on a standard window in the 24″–36″ wide range. Prices include the shade itself but not installation labor.

| Shade Type | Price Per Window | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Basic roller shades | $150–$350 | Light-filtering polyester fabric; most popular entry-level option |
| Solar shades | $200–$450 | Openness factor matters: 3% blocks more light than 10% |
| Blackout roller shades | $200–$400 | Dense foam-backed or triple-weave fabric; essential for bedrooms |
| Cellular/honeycomb shades | $250–$500 | Single-cell on the lower end; double-cell adds ~$60–$80 |
| Roman shades | $300–$700 | Complex construction; fabric quality drives the upper range |
| Motorized roller shades | $400–$800 | Battery-powered at the low end; Wi-Fi/smart-home at the top |

*Prices are consistent with data from [HomeGuide's 2026 window treatment cost guide](https://homeguide.com/costs/window-treatment-cost), [Blindsgalore's custom shade pricing breakdown](https://www.blindsgalore.com/blog/index.php/beyond-basics/how-much-do-custom-blinds-cost/), and [SenseBlinds' materials and labor analysis](https://senseblinds.com/blogs/news/breaking-down-custom-shade-pricing-materials-motors-labor-shipping-fees).*

A few things to keep in mind as you scan that table: "custom" means made-to-your-exact-measurement, not a mass-produced shade in a standard size. That precision is precisely why the prices start higher than what you'd grab off a shelf at a home improvement store.

## What Drives the Price Up (or Down)

Size is the single biggest variable. A 72″-wide sliding glass door shade can cost 60–80% more than a standard 36″ bedroom window in the same fabric, simply because it requires more material and a heavier-duty hardware system. For full details on large-window pricing, see our guide on [custom roller shades for large windows](/blog/custom-roller-shades-large-windows).

**Fabric type** is the second-biggest driver. Basic polyester runs $40–$100 in material cost alone, while linen blends jump to $100–$250, and specialty textured weaves or designer fabrics can reach $300 or more per window according to [SenseBlinds' pricing breakdown](https://senseblinds.com/blogs/news/breaking-down-custom-shade-pricing-materials-motors-labor-shipping-fees).

**Motorization** can nearly double your per-window cost. Entry-level DC motors add $100–$200 per shade. RF (radio frequency) remote-controlled motors run $200–$350. Smart Wi-Fi motors with app control or voice assistant integration are $350–$700 per shade on top of the base shade price. For a full breakdown of whether motorization is worth the cost, see our [motorized roller shades guide](/blog/motorized-roller-shades-worth-it).

**Inside vs. outside mount** matters more than most people expect. An inside-mount shade must be cut to a precise deduction (often 3/8″ narrower than your frame measurement) and requires more exact installation. Outside-mount shades cover a larger area, which means more material and a slightly higher price, but they're more forgiving to install. For a detailed comparison, see our article on [inside mount vs. outside mount roller shades](/blog/inside-mount-vs-outside-mount-roller-shades).

**Quantity discounts** are real. Most manufacturers offer 10–20% off when you're covering 10 or more windows at once, according to [Lock and Tech's motorized shade pricing guide](https://lockandtech.com/how-much-do-motorized-shades-cost/). Ordering whole-home is almost always cheaper per window than ordering one room at a time.

## The Hidden Costs Nobody Talks About

The shade price you see on a product page is rarely your final number. Here's what typically gets added:

**Professional measuring:** Some companies offer free in-home measurement consultations. Others charge $50–$150 for the visit, though many will credit that fee toward your purchase. If you're ordering motorized shades or have unusual window shapes (arched, angled, or bay windows), professional measuring isn't optional — a wrong measurement on a custom order means you're eating the cost of a new shade. Learn how to measure correctly yourself in our [step-by-step window measuring guide](/blog/how-to-measure-windows-roller-shades).

**Installation labor:** Plan on $75–$150 per window for professional installation, with standard single-shade installs on the lower end and motorized setups on the higher end. [SenseBlinds' labor breakdown](https://senseblinds.com/blogs/news/breaking-down-custom-shade-pricing-materials-motors-labor-shipping-fees) puts standard installation at $50–$100 per window, with motorized shade setup running $100–$200+. [HomeAdvisor's 2025 data](https://www.homeadvisor.com/cost/doors-and-windows/install-window-treatments/) confirms installers typically charge $35–$100 per hour, with each window taking up to two hours for complex pieces.

**Shipping on custom orders:** Custom shades can't be returned to a warehouse shelf, so manufacturers build in packaging and handling charges. Flat-rate shipping is common, but for large orders with heavy blackout linings or motorized tracks, variable shipping can add $50–$200+ to your total. Some retailers offer free shipping above a spend threshold — worth asking about before you finalize your order.

**Rush fees:** Most custom shade manufacturers operate on a 5–10 business day production timeline. If you need shades faster — say, before a move-in date — expedited production adds $50 to several hundred dollars depending on order size, according to [Blindsgalore's cost considerations guide](https://www.blindsgalore.com/blog/index.php/blinds-basics/cost-considerations-for-custom-window-shades/).

**Removal of old treatments:** If a pro is installing your new shades, ask upfront whether removing and hauling away your old blinds is included. Many installers charge $20–$50 per window for this separately.

## Custom vs. Ready-Made: The 10-Year Math

Here's where the conversation gets interesting. A cheap $40 roller shade from a big-box store sounds like a deal — and it is, until you run the numbers over a decade.

According to [AAA Blind & Shutter Factory's cost comparison](https://aaablindandshutterfactory.com/custom-vs-ready-made-window-treatments-cost-guide/), ready-made window treatments typically last 2–5 years, while custom-made treatments last 10–15 years. Here's what that actually means for your wallet on a single window:

**Ready-made approach (10 years):**
- $40 shade at year 0
- $40 replacement at year 3–4
- $40 replacement at year 6–7
- $40 replacement at year 9–10
- **Total: ~$160 per window** — plus your time installing each one, plus the annoyance of a shade that was never quite the right width anyway

**Custom shade approach (10 years):**
- $250 custom roller shade at year 0, professionally installed
- One-time cost, no replacements needed within the decade
- **Total: $250–$400 per window** (including installation) — for a product that fits perfectly and doesn't let light bleed around the edges

The gap narrows considerably when you factor in energy savings. Custom cellular shades, for example, can provide up to R-4 insulation and reduce heat transfer through windows by 20–30%, per [AAA Blind's analysis](https://aaablindandshutterfactory.com/custom-vs-ready-made-window-treatments-cost-guide/). For a home in a climate with real heating and cooling costs, that adds up.

For most homeowners, the 10-year math lands in favor of quality custom shades — especially in high-use rooms where cheap treatments will degrade the fastest.

## How to Save on Custom Shades Without Sacrificing Quality

**Order your whole home at once.** This is the single most effective way to reduce per-window costs. Manufacturers routinely offer volume pricing at 10+ windows, and you'll pay one installation fee rather than multiple trip charges.

**Choose manual operation where it makes sense.** Motorization is genuinely useful in rooms where shades are hard to reach — above a kitchen sink, high clerestory windows, above a staircase. In a bedroom where you'll pull a chain twice a day, you're adding $200–$400 per window in motor cost for marginal convenience. Save motorization for the windows where it actually solves a problem.

**Skip the premium valance.** Decorative cassettes and fabric valances can add $30–$100 per shade. They look polished, but a clean fascia or no fascia at all is equally elegant in most modern interiors.

**Choose a lighter fabric in rooms that don't need blackout.** The jump from a light-filtering polyester to a blackout lining adds $40–$120 per window, per [SenseBlinds' specialty features breakdown](https://senseblinds.com/blogs/news/breaking-down-custom-shade-pricing-materials-motors-labor-shipping-fees). Reserve blackout fabric for bedrooms and media rooms; light-filtering is perfectly functional in living spaces and offices. For nursery-specific blackout recommendations, see our [nursery blackout shade guide](/blog/blackout-shades-nursery-cordless).

**Time your order strategically.** Most window covering companies run promotions in January (post-holiday) and late summer (pre-fall refresh). Ordering during a sale can save 15–25% across a whole-home project.

## The Bottom Line

Custom window shade pricing in 2026 spans a wide range — from around $150 per window for a basic manual roller shade up to $800 or more for a motorized smart shade with premium fabric. The number that matters for your project depends on your window sizes, how many you're covering, the fabric and control options you choose, and whether you're including installation.

What you shouldn't do is make your decision based on the sticker price of a ready-made shade at a home improvement store. When you factor in replacement cycles, fit quality, and energy performance, custom shades are frequently the more cost-effective choice over a 10-year horizon.

Get quotes from at least two or three manufacturers, ask for an itemized breakdown (fabric, hardware, shipping, installation), and don't be afraid to ask about volume pricing if you're covering multiple rooms.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How much should I budget per window for custom shades?

For most standard windows, budget $200–$450 for the shade itself. Add $75–$150 per window if you're using a professional installer. Motorized options push per-window totals to $600–$1,000+ once you include the motor, installation, and any smart-home integration. At World Wide Shades, our team is happy to provide a detailed quote so you know exactly what to expect before placing an order.

### Are there hidden fees when ordering custom window shades?

Yes, and they're worth asking about in advance. Common add-ons include professional measuring fees ($50–$150, often credited back), shipping and handling, removal of old window treatments ($20–$50 per window), and rush production fees if you need your order quickly. Reputable companies will itemize these clearly in your quote.

### Do custom shades cost more than ready-made shades in the long run?

Not necessarily. Ready-made shades in the $20–$80 range typically last 2–5 years before fading, warping, or breaking, per data from [AAA Blind & Shutter Factory](https://aaablindandshutterfactory.com/custom-vs-ready-made-window-treatments-cost-guide/). Replacing them two or three times over a decade often costs more — and delivers less — than a single set of well-made custom shades built to last 10–15 years.

### Does ordering more windows at once save money?

Yes, significantly. Volume discounts of 10–20% are standard across most custom shade manufacturers when ordering 10 or more windows. You'll also consolidate installation costs into a single trip rather than paying per-visit charges across multiple orders.
