Sliding glass doors are one of the hardest windows to cover well. They're wide, high-traffic, and need to open easily every day. The wrong treatment doesn't just look bad — it gets in the way of your life. You're carrying groceries, letting the dog out, or stepping onto the patio a dozen times a day. The last thing you want is a shade that tangles, jams, or forces you to wrestle with cords every time. At World Wide Shades, we've outfitted hundreds of sliding glass doors and know exactly where standard solutions fall short — and what actually works.

The good news: a handful of window treatments are genuinely designed for this challenge. Here are the five best options, ranked by how well they actually perform on sliding glass doors — plus what to avoid and how to measure correctly.

## What Makes Sliding Doors Different

Before picking a treatment, it helps to understand why sliding doors are a category of their own.

**They're wide.** A standard sliding glass door runs 60 to 72 inches wide. Larger patio configurations stretch to 96, 108, or even 144 inches. That rules out most standard off-the-shelf blinds immediately — you need a custom solution built for the span.

**They're used constantly.** A bedroom window might go weeks without being opened. Your patio door gets used multiple times every day. Fragile mechanisms, delicate fabrics on pull-cords, and treatments that require two hands to operate will wear out — or wear on you — quickly.

**They need to clear the opening.** A horizontal blind on a regular window lifts straight up and stays out of the way. On a sliding door, you need the treatment to stack or retract to the side so the door panel can actually slide. Anything that doesn't move horizontally creates an obstacle.

**Horizontal operation is the natural fit.** Treatments that slide left or right match the door's own movement. This is why vertical-format window treatments — panel tracks, vertical cellular shades, vertical blinds — dominate this category.

## The 5 Best Options, Ranked

### 1. Panel Track Blinds (Top Pick)

Panel track blinds are the best all-around solution for sliding glass doors, and it's not particularly close.

Instead of individual slats or a shade that rolls up, panel tracks use wide, flat fabric panels — typically 18 to 24 inches each — that glide along a ceiling- or wall-mounted aluminum track. When you want access to the door, the panels slide and stack neatly to one side (or split to both sides). When closed, they create a seamless, flat wall of fabric across the entire opening.

What makes them stand out:

- **Clean, modern look** that works in contemporary and transitional interiors alike
- **Hundreds of fabric options** — from sheer light-filtering weaves to full blackout materials, natural woven textures, and solar screen fabrics
- **Stacks completely clear the opening**, leaving nothing in the way of the door
- **Quiet, smooth operation** — the wheeled track system glides without the rattling or clicking of traditional vanes
- **Custom widths up to 144 inches or more** make them viable for even the largest patio door configurations

The stack direction is fully configurable: left stack, right stack, split stack (panels part in the middle), or center stack. For a door where you typically exit on one side, a single-direction stack keeps the active side completely unobstructed.

One thing to know: panel track blinds work best as an outside mount. They need wall space on the stacking side, so plan for 12–18 inches of clear wall to accommodate the panels when open. If you're tight on wall space, split-stack minimizes this.

**Best for:** Modern and contemporary homes, large openings, anyone who wants a clean architectural look.

### 2. Vertical Cellular Shades

If energy efficiency is a priority, vertical cellular shades earn the second spot.

These are built on the same honeycomb principle as standard cellular shades — insulating air pockets trap heat in winter and block solar gain in summer — but oriented vertically so they can stack horizontally like panel tracks. According to [Bali Blinds](https://baliblinds.costco.com/shades/vertical-cellular-shades/), vertical cellular shades reduce heat transfer by up to 64% compared to non-cellular shades.

That insulation benefit matters most on large glass openings. A sliding glass door is often the biggest thermal vulnerability in a room — and in climates with hot summers or cold winters, the energy savings add up fast. The double-cell (also called honeycomb-within-a-honeycomb) construction available from manufacturers like [Hunter Douglas](https://www.hunterdouglas.com/stories/buyers-guides/window-treatments-patio-sliding-glass-doors) provides the highest insulation rating.

They're available in light-filtering and blackout fabrics, and the compact stack keeps the door opening clear. As a bonus, if you have cellular shades on other windows in the room, you can match them with a vertical version on the patio door for a cohesive, coordinated look.

**Best for:** Hot or cold climates, energy-conscious homeowners, rooms where matching other cellular shades matters.

### 3. Roller Shades

Roller shades are a strong choice for homeowners who want the simplest, most streamlined look possible.

A single large roller shade spans the entire width of the door and rolls up into a compact tube at the top. When fully raised, the profile is minimal — just a narrow tube and the fabric rolls nearly out of sight. There's no visible hardware, no hanging panels, no slats. For a very clean, modern aesthetic, it's hard to beat.

The main trade-off: because it's a single shade, you have to raise the entire thing to access the door. This is why **motorization is strongly recommended** for roller shades on sliding doors (more on that below). With a motorized roller shade, raising and lowering is a one-button or one-voice-command operation, which eliminates the daily friction entirely. For a full breakdown of motorized options, costs, and compatibility, see our [motorized roller shades guide](/blog/motorized-roller-shades-worth-it).

Roller shades come in light-filtering, blackout, and solar screen versions. Solar screen fabrics are particularly popular on patio doors because they reduce glare and block UV rays while maintaining a view of the outdoors. For example, a 5% openness solar screen blocks 95% of UV rays while still letting you see outside during daylight hours.

An alternative configuration: two side-by-side roller shades instead of one wide one. This lets you raise only the half covering the active door panel, which is a practical middle ground if motorization isn't in the budget.

**Best for:** Minimalist and modern interiors, motorized setups, spaces where a clean visual line matters most.

### 4. Vertical Blinds

Vertical blinds are the classic choice — and they're classic for good reasons.

Individual PVC, fabric, or faux-wood vanes hang from a headrail and tilt to control light. They slide to the side to clear the door. They're durable, easy to wipe clean, resistant to moisture (PVC vanes handle humidity without warping), and typically the most budget-friendly option in this category. For a rental property, a high-traffic family space, or anywhere you just need something functional and low-maintenance, vertical blinds remain a sensible call.

The honest caveat: they look dated in most contemporary interiors. The individual vanes can rattle in air conditioning drafts, they're noisier than panel tracks, and fabric vanes can eventually curl at the bottom with heavy use. They also offer less fabric variety than panel tracks.

That said, [vertical blinds do offer something panel tracks don't](https://www.verticalvics.com/blog/vertical-blinds-vs-panel-track-shades-which-is-better-for-patio-doors): tilting slats for nuanced light control. You can angle the vanes to redirect sunlight without fully opening or closing the treatment — useful in rooms where glare management is important throughout the day.

**Best for:** Budget-conscious buyers, rental properties, high-humidity spaces like laundry rooms with patio access, anyone who needs precise light-angle control.

### 5. Drapery + Shade Layering

For the most decorative, high-end look, layering floor-to-ceiling drapery over a light-filtering shade behind it gives you maximum design versatility.

The setup: a roller shade or sheer panel track installed closest to the glass for light filtration, with floor-to-ceiling drapery panels on a bypass or ceiling-mounted track in front. During the day, you draw the drapery back and let the sheer shade soften the light. At night, you close the drapery for full privacy and a warm, finished look. If you want to block the light entirely, a blackout shade sits behind it all. For maximum darkness, see our guide on [eliminating light gaps in blackout shades](/blog/blackout-shades-no-light-gaps).

This approach works especially well in formal living rooms, dining rooms, and primary bedrooms where the patio door is as much an architectural feature as a functional one. It's also the best option if you want the sliding door to look like a dramatic floor-to-ceiling window from inside.

The operational requirement: the drapery must hang on a bypass or gliding track (not a rod with rings or rod-pocket panels) so it opens cleanly to the side without tangling or sagging. A proper bypass drapery track is designed for exactly this.

**Best for:** Formal spaces, primary bedrooms, homeowners who want a layered, designer look with maximum light versatility.

## What NOT to Use on Sliding Doors

**Horizontal blinds (2″ aluminum or wood blinds).** These are designed to raise and lower vertically. To access your door, you'd have to raise the entire blind every time. On a 7-foot-tall sliding door, that means lifting a large, heavy blind multiple times a day. It's inconvenient at best, and the mechanism will wear out faster than it should. Horizontal blinds simply don't operate in the right direction for this application — [this is a consistent recommendation across industry sources](https://www.blindsgalore.com/blog/index.php/blinds-basics/vertical-vs-panel-track-vs-horizontal-blinds-for-sliding-patio-doors/).

**Rod-pocket curtains.** Rod-pocket curtains are designed to hang decoratively, not to be opened and closed repeatedly. The fabric bunches up on the rod every time you push it aside, which creates a frustrating operation and damages the fabric at the pocket over time. If you want drapery on a sliding door, use a proper gliding or bypass track system — not a rod.

**Roman shades without motorization.** Roman shades can work on sliding doors in theory, but they stack with significant bulk when raised and require reaching up to operate a cord or chain. On a 7-foot door, cordless operation usually isn't reachable without a step stool. If you love the look of Roman shades, a motorized version solves the access problem — but at that point, a motorized roller shade gives you a similar result with less stack.

## Motorized vs. Manual for Patio Doors

For most window treatments, the manual vs. motorized decision comes down to personal preference and budget. For sliding glass doors, **motorization is a genuine quality-of-life upgrade worth serious consideration**.

Think about when you use your patio door most:
- Carrying groceries from the car
- Letting a dog out (and back in, and back out)
- Moving in and out while cooking or entertaining
- Stepping out with hands full of plates, drinks, or garden tools

In every one of these situations, having two free hands matters. A motorized shade raises or lowers with a tap on your phone, a remote, or a voice command to Alexa or Google Home. Many motorized systems also support schedules — you can set the shades to raise automatically at sunrise and lower at sunset, without touching anything.

Modern motorized shades for sliding doors are also battery-powered or hardwired depending on your installation, and they integrate with [smart home platforms including Apple HomeKit, Google Home, and Amazon Alexa](https://www.blinds.com/motorized). Battery-operated motors typically last 1–3 years on a charge and are rechargeable via USB.

If budget is a constraint, motorization is most valuable on wide roller shades (heavy and awkward to raise manually) and panel tracks on very large openings. Vertical blinds and panel tracks on standard 72-inch doors are manageable manually. ## Measuring Tips for Sliding Doors

Measuring a sliding glass door correctly is the most important step in getting a custom treatment that fits and functions properly.

**Always use a steel tape measure.** Fabric or cloth measuring tapes flex and give inaccurate readings on wide spans.

**Measure three times.** For width, measure at the top, middle, and bottom of the opening. For height, measure at the left, center, and right. Openings are rarely perfectly square.

**Outside mount is almost always the right choice for sliding doors.** Unlike a standard window with significant frame depth, sliding door frames are typically shallow — often not enough depth to accommodate the headrail of a vertical treatment inside the frame. Outside mount also lets you extend the treatment above and to the sides of the door for better light blockage and a more dramatic look.

For an outside mount:
- **Width:** Measure the total width of the frame you want to cover, then add 2–3 inches on each side for overlap and light control. For a 72-inch door frame, ordering at 78–80 inches total width is typical.
- **Height:** Measure from your desired mounting point (usually 2–3 inches above the top of the frame, or at the ceiling for a floor-to-ceiling look) down to the floor. For treatments that don't reach the floor, measure to 1 inch above the floor or door sill.

**Account for stack space.** Panel tracks and vertical cellular shades need wall space on the stacking side when open. Measure how much clear wall you have on the side where panels will stack before choosing a stack direction.

**Check door hardware clearance.** The door handle and lock mechanism typically project 2–4 inches from the door surface. For inside-mount or close-clearance outside-mount treatments, confirm the headrail won't interfere with handle operation.

When in doubt, contact your window treatment manufacturer with your raw measurements. Custom fabricators can work through the geometry with you before cutting anything. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can you put a single shade across an entire 8-foot sliding glass door?**

Yes — panel tracks, roller shades, and vertical cellular shades can all be fabricated in widths up to 144 inches or more. A roller shade spanning the full width is a popular choice for a seamless look, though motorization is recommended at that scale. Panel tracks handle large widths naturally because multiple panels distribute the coverage. Our team at World Wide Shades has outfitted many 8-foot and wider sliding glass doors — motorization is something we always recommend at that scale for ease of daily use.

**What's the difference between panel track blinds and vertical blinds?**

Panel tracks use wide, flat fabric panels (typically 18–24 inches wide) that slide on a ceiling track — no tilting vanes, just smooth open/close operation. Vertical blinds use individual narrow vanes (typically 3.5 inches wide) that can both slide and tilt to adjust light angles. Panel tracks look more modern and operate more quietly. Vertical blinds offer more nuanced light control and are generally less expensive.

**Do I need to measure the door panel separately from the fixed panel?**

For most treatments (panel tracks, a single spanning roller shade, vertical cellular shades), you measure the entire door opening as one unit — both the sliding panel and the stationary panel together. The treatment covers the whole span and stacks to one side. If you're installing two separate treatments (one per panel), measure each panel's width individually and account for overlap at the center.

**How do I keep a large roller shade from being difficult to raise and lower manually?**

For roller shades wider than about 72 inches, a continuous cord loop system (rather than a standard spring-assisted cordless mechanism) gives you better mechanical advantage and control. That said, at 84 inches or wider, motorization is the more practical long-term solution. The weight of a wide, long shade adds up, and motor-assisted operation keeps daily use effortless year after year.
