## What “true blackout” actually means
Most people buy “blackout” shades expecting a perfectly dark room. Then they see bright light lines at the sides, a glow at the top, or spill at the bottom.

True blackout is a **system**, not a single fabric choice:

- blackout fabric (or a blackout liner)
- the right mount type and overlap
- top and side light control
- bottom rail alignment

## Checklist item 3A: Side channel vs wider shade—how to choose
### Choose a wider outside mount when
- you want better blackout but don’t want visible channels
- your window is standard and the wall surface is even

### Choose side channels when
- you’re very light sensitive
- you have strong sunrise exposure
- you’re fighting streetlights or security lights
- you’re a shift worker sleeping during the day

Side channels are often the closest practical step toward “no light anywhere.”

## Checklist item 2: Pick the mount that reduces light leaks
### Outside mount is usually the blackout winner
Outside mount lets you overlap the window opening and reduce side gaps.

### Inside mount can work, but it’s harder
Inside mount looks clean, but you’ll typically have visible side gaps because the shade must clear the window frame.

## Checklist item 3: Plan side coverage (where most “blackout” fails)
Side light leaks are the #1 complaint.

Your main options:

### Option A: Add extra width (simple and effective)
For outside mounts, add enough width so the fabric overlaps the glass and frame.

### Option B: Use side channels (closest to “no light anywhere”)
Side channels guide the shade and block side light. They’re especially useful for:

- streetlights
- sunrise-facing bedrooms
- shift workers
- light-sensitive sleepers

Solutions include:

- cassette/headrail that fits tightly
- valance that covers the top light line
- careful installation (level and tight to the mounting surface)

## Checklist item 8: Verify your plan with a “light test”
After installation:

1) close the shade during the brightest part of day
2) turn off all interior lights
3) check top, sides, and bottom for spill
4) adjust limits and mounting if needed

If you still see gaps, revisit side coverage or channels.

## FAQ: Bedroom blackout checklist
### Why do blackout shades still let light in?
Because most light leaks come from the sides and top, not through the fabric. Blackout fabric blocks the material, but gaps around the shade still pass light.

### Is outside mount always better for blackout?
Usually, yes—outside mount lets you overlap the opening and reduce side gaps. Inside mount looks cleaner but typically has more light leakage.

### What’s the best way to eliminate side light leaks?
Side channels are the most effective, followed by wider outside mounts that overlap the frame.

### Do I need a valance if I have blackout fabric?
A valance or cassette can help cover the top gap and improve the finished look, especially if the window wall isn’t perfectly flat.

### Can I get blackout performance and still have a stylish bedroom?
Yes—choose a fabric that matches your decor, then build the blackout “system” with the right mount, overlap, and optional channels.
