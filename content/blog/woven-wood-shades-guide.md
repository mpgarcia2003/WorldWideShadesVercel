## Why woven wood is back (and why it fits modern design)

Woven wood shades add texture—something modern spaces often lack—without introducing heavy drapery.
They pair especially well with minimalist interiors because the pattern is organic, not busy.
Most woven woods use bamboo, grasses, reeds, or jute-like fibers; the material variation is part of the charm.

## Light control options: unlined, privacy-lined, and blackout-lined

Unlined woven woods create a warm, dappled light effect, but privacy depends on weave tightness.
A privacy liner makes the shade more opaque and consistent, reducing silhouettes at night.
A blackout liner is best for bedrooms, but note: side gaps still matter—outside mount or add drapery panels for true darkness.

## Measuring and mounting: depth and clearance you can’t ignore

Woven woods are bulkier than roller shades. Inside-mount requires enough depth so the shade doesn’t protrude awkwardly.
If your window has shallow depth, outside-mount often looks more intentional.
Plan for stack height: woven woods ‘stack’ higher when raised, which can block a portion of the glass.

## Where woven wood performs best

Dining rooms and living rooms: texture and warmth.
Bedrooms: with a liner, or layered with curtains.

## FAQ


### How do I measure for woven wood shades: natural texture for modern homes?

Measure width and height in three places, use the smallest width for inside-mount, and always double-check depth and obstructions like handles or trim.

### Should I choose light filtering or blackout?

Choose light filtering for living spaces where you want daylight and glare control; choose blackout for bedrooms, nurseries, media rooms, or any room where sleep or presentations matter.

### What is a realistic budget per window?

For most custom roller-style shades, plan roughly $120–$420 per window, with motorization adding about $250–$650 when desired.

### Can I order samples first?

Yes—samples are the fastest way to confirm color, texture, and light behavior in your home. Order them from /swatches and test morning vs. afternoon light.

## Pro measurement checklist (print this before you measure)

Write down each window name (for example: ‘Primary Bedroom – Left’) and record width at the top/middle/bottom and height at left/center/right.
Note obstructions: cranks, alarms, trim projections, tile returns, or anything that could interfere with a bracket.
If you’re outside-mounting, sketch where the brackets will land and confirm you have solid material for anchors.
Finally, verify your control side (left/right) based on furniture placement and outlet location if you plan motorization.
