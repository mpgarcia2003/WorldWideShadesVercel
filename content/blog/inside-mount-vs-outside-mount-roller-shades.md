Before you order roller shades, you need to answer one critical question: inside or outside mount? Get this wrong and your shades won't fit, won't function, and won't look right — even if the fabric is perfect. This single decision affects how the shade installs, how much light it blocks, and whether it physically fits your window at all. At World Wide Shades, we help customers make this exact call every day, and a few key measurements usually settle the question quickly.

The good news: once you understand what each mount type does and how to measure for it, the choice is straightforward.

## What Is Inside Mount?

An **inside mount** (IM) roller shade installs directly within the window frame opening. The brackets sit at the top corners of the recess, so the shade rolls up and down entirely inside the frame — flush with or slightly behind the wall surface.

The result is a sleek, built-in appearance that keeps your window trim and architectural details fully visible. For windows with attractive wood casings or modern minimalist frames, inside mount is the natural choice.

**Depth requirements vary by configuration:**
- **Minimum secure mount:** approximately **3/4″–1.5″** of frame depth for a basic roller without a valance or fascia ([The Shade Store](https://static.theshadestore.com/s3/theshadestore/cms/files/Roller_Shades-2.pdf), [SelectBlinds](https://www.selectblinds.com/measure/measure-roller-shades.html))
- **Flush inside mount** (headrail fully recessed): **2.25″–3.375″** depending on bracket style ([The Shade Store](https://static.theshadestore.com/s3/theshadestore/cms/files/Roller_Shades-2.pdf))
- **With a fascia or cassette valance:** flush installation requires **3″–4.375″** ([SelectBlinds](https://www.selectblinds.com/measure/measure-roller-shades.html))

One caveat: inside mount shades have a small **light gap** on each side — typically 3/8″–1″ per side — because the fabric must be slightly narrower than the opening to roll freely. For blackout applications, side channels are needed to seal these gaps ([Quiltcraft](https://www.quiltcraft.com/blog/roller-shades-inside-mount-vs-outside-mount)). For complete strategies to eliminate these gaps, see our [blackout shades light gap guide](/blog/blackout-shades-no-light-gaps).

## What Is Outside Mount?

An **outside mount** (OM) roller shade attaches to the wall, window molding, or ceiling above and around the frame. The shade covers the entire window opening plus a planned overlap on all sides.

There is **no minimum depth requirement** for outside mount — making it the default solution whenever a window fails the depth check ([The Shade Store](https://static.theshadestore.com/s3/theshadestore/cms/files/Roller_Shades-2.pdf)). The brackets mount to a flat wall surface, so frame depth is irrelevant.

**Standard overlap guidelines:**
- **Width:** add 1.5″–3″ beyond the frame on each side (3″–6″ total) for light control ([A Shade Above](https://www.ashadeabovewindowfashions.com/blog/tips-and-tricks-for-selecting-inside-vs-outside-mount-blinds/), [Bali Blinds](https://www.baliblinds.com/globalassets/3.-documents/measuring-guides/bali-roller-solar-shades-measuring-guide.pdf))
- **Height:** mount 3″ above the opening to accommodate hardware; extend 2″–3″ below the sill for full coverage
- **Flat mounting surface:** at least 1.25″–2″ of flat vertical wall above the window is needed for bracket installation ([Award Blinds](https://www.awardblinds.com/measuring-guides/premium-blackout-vinyl-roller-shades.html))

Outside mount shades can also make a window look taller and wider — a useful visual trick in smaller rooms.

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| Feature | Inside Mount | Outside Mount |
|---|---|---|
| **Appearance** | Sleek, built-in; trim stays visible | Bold, dramatic; covers molding and frame |
| **Light blocking** | Side gaps (3/8″–1″ per side); blackout needs side channels | Minimal gaps; best for true blackout |
| **Window depth required** | Min. 3/4″–1.5″ (secure); 2.25″–4.375″+ (flush) | None |
| **Ease of installation** | Requires depth check and obstruction clearance | More forgiving; flexible mounting position |
| **Cost** | Standard | May need extra fabric for overlap area |
| **Best for** | Deep frames, modern aesthetics, exposed trim | Shallow frames, blackout needs, visually larger windows |

## When to Choose Inside Mount

Inside mount works best when:

- **Frame depth is 3″ or more** — easily accommodates most configurations including fascias and cassettes
- **You want trim to remain visible** — inside mount leaves all molding and architectural detail in full view
- **You prefer a clean, modern look** — the shade appears built into the window itself
- **Windows are standard rectangular shapes** — consistent, square frames make inside mount easy to specify
- **Light filtering (not blackout) is the goal** — sheer or light-filtering fabrics work well inside mounted, since side gaps aren't a significant concern

---

## When to Choose Outside Mount

Outside mount is the better choice when:

- **Shallow frames under 2″ deep** don't provide enough depth for bracket installation
- **Maximum light blocking is required** — bedrooms, nurseries, and home theaters benefit from edge-to-edge coverage; outside mount is the industry standard for blackout applications ([Bali Blinds](https://www.baliblinds.com/globalassets/3.-documents/measuring-guides/bali-roller-solar-shades-measuring-guide.pdf), [Granite State Parcel](https://gsparcel.com/insights/how-do-you-measure-for-roller-shades/))
- **You want the window to appear larger** — mounting the shade high and wide draws the eye upward and outward
- **The frame is uneven or out-of-square** — if diagonal measurements differ by more than 1/2″, outside mount covers imperfections completely ([AAA Blind & Shutters](https://aaablindandshutterfactory.com/roller-shades-accurate-measurement-tips/))
- **Obstructions block the recess** — cranks, handles, tilt rods, or HVAC registers inside the frame

For nurseries and blackout bedrooms in particular, outside mount should be the default. See our [nursery blackout shade guide](/blog/blackout-shades-nursery-cordless) for room-specific recommendations.

## The Depth Check — How to Know If Inside Mount Works

Checking window depth is the most critical step before specifying inside mount.

**Step 1: Use a rigid steel tape measure** — not a cloth tape.

**Step 2: Measure at three points.** Measure from the front face of the frame straight back to the glass at the left, center, and right of the window.

**Step 3: Use the smallest number.** The shallowest measurement is your limiting dimension.

**Step 4: Compare to product specs.** Minimum depth thresholds vary by configuration:

| Configuration | Secure Inside Mount | Flush Inside Mount |
|---|---|---|
| Basic roller, no valance | 3/4″–1.5″ | 2.25″–3″ |
| Cassette valance | 1.5″–2.125″ | 3.25″–3.75″ |
| Metal fascia (3″–4″) | 2″–2.5″ | 3.375″–4.375″ |

*Ranges based on specs from [The Shade Store](https://static.theshadestore.com/s3/theshadestore/cms/files/Roller_Shades-2.pdf), [SelectBlinds](https://www.selectblinds.com/measure/measure-roller-shades.html), and [Bali Blinds](https://www.baliblinds.com/globalassets/3.-documents/measuring-guides/bali-roller-solar-shades-measuring-guide.pdf). Always verify against your specific product's spec sheet.*

**Step 5: Check for obstructions.** Window cranks, lock handles, and tilt rods reduce usable depth. A crank that protrudes 2″ into the recess cuts your available depth by 2″ ([AAA Blind & Shutters](https://aaablindandshutterfactory.com/roller-shades-accurate-measurement-tips/)).

If depth meets the minimum for your chosen product, inside mount works. If not, go outside mount.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Ordering inside mount without checking depth.** This is the most common — and most expensive — error. Always measure depth before configuring your shade.

**Not adding overlap for outside mount.** Shades ordered at the exact window width will still have light gaps. The fabric is typically 1″–1.5″ narrower than the headrail. Add 1.5″–3″ per side beyond the frame so the fabric actually covers the opening ([Award Blinds](https://www.awardblinds.com/measuring-guides/premium-blackout-vinyl-roller-shades.html)).

**Forgetting handles and locks.** A window crank or lock handle inside the recess can prevent a shade from rolling down fully or block bracket installation entirely. Always note all hardware obstructions when measuring.

**Making your own deductions for inside mount.** Manufacturers apply factory deductions to inside mount orders automatically. If you subtract from your width measurement and the factory subtracts again, your shade will be too narrow. Provide the exact opening measurement and let the manufacturer handle clearances ([AAA Blind & Shutters](https://aaablindandshutterfactory.com/roller-shades-accurate-measurement-tips/)).

## Can You Mix Mount Types in One Room?

Yes — and it is more common than you might think.

A room with different window types frequently benefits from a mixed approach. A standard double-hung window with a 4″ deep frame might take inside mount, while a shallow casement window in the same room goes outside mount because the crank hardware takes up too much recess depth.

The shades still look cohesive as long as:
- The **fabric is the same** across all windows
- The **roller height is consistent** — mount outside shades high enough that the bottom hem aligns with adjacent inside-mounted shades
- The **hardware finish matches** across both configurations

Mixed mounting strategies look intentional, not patchwork, when the fabric and hardware are coordinated.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Can any roller shade be inside or outside mounted?**
Most roller shades support both configurations. The mount type affects hardware and dimensions, not the fabric. Always confirm your specific product supports inside mount and verify the depth requirement for that product line. At World Wide Shades, we note the minimum depth requirement for each product in our catalog; if your frame is borderline, we'll recommend the right approach before you order.

**Q: Will an inside mount roller shade block all the light?**
Not on its own. Inside mount shades have a light gap of 3/8″–1″ per side. For blackout performance, add side channels (light-blocking tracks) that close this gap, or choose outside mount instead ([Quiltcraft](https://www.quiltcraft.com/blog/roller-shades-inside-mount-vs-outside-mount), [Bali Blinds](https://www.baliblinds.com/globalassets/3.-documents/measuring-guides/bali-roller-solar-shades-measuring-guide.pdf)).

**Q: How much should an outside mount shade overlap the window?**
A standard rule is 1.5″–3″ of overlap beyond the frame on each side, plus 3″ above the frame for bracket clearance. For blackout rooms, lean toward the larger overlap to minimize edge light leakage ([Bali Blinds](https://www.baliblinds.com/globalassets/3.-documents/measuring-guides/bali-roller-solar-shades-measuring-guide.pdf)).

**Q: What if my window is right on the depth borderline?**
Check whether you need a flush mount or just a secure mount. A secure inside mount (headrail protrudes slightly past the frame face) requires significantly less depth than a flush mount. You can also opt for an exposed roll with no fascia or cassette to reduce the depth requirement — or go outside mount for peace of mind.
