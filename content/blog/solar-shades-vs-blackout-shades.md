Solar shades and blackout shades are the two most popular roller shade categories — and they do almost opposite things. One is engineered to let diffused light in while killing glare and UV exposure. The other is engineered to stop light completely. Choosing the wrong one is a frustrating and expensive mistake, so this guide walks you through exactly what each shade does, where each belongs, and how to decide for your specific windows.

## What Solar Shades Actually Do

A solar shade is woven from an open-weave fabric — typically polyester coated in vinyl — that acts like a mesh screen across your window. The fabric has an "openness factor" rated by percentage: a 1% openness fabric blocks more light and heat than a 10% openness fabric, which lets in more daylight and gives you a clearer view to the outside.

Here's what that looks like in practice:

- **1% openness** — strong glare reduction, reduced but still visible outside view, best for south- and west-facing windows with intense afternoon sun
- **3% openness** — the most common choice; balances glare control with a clear outward view; blocks roughly 97% of UV rays
- **5% openness** — bright, airy feel; still blocks 95% of UV; good for north-facing rooms or spaces where you want maximum natural light
- **10% openness** — very light filtering, best for rooms where you want maximum transparency and outdoor connection

The critical thing solar shades do not do: they do not provide privacy at night. Once interior lights are on after dark, anyone outside can see in through most solar fabrics. If nighttime privacy matters, you'll want to [design your custom shade](/builder) with that in mind — either pairing a solar with a blackout in a dual-roller configuration, or choosing a room-darkening alternative.

Solar roller shades typically cost between **$80 and $250 per window** for custom sizes, depending on fabric openness, width, and hardware finish. That range reflects standard single-roller configurations; motorized upgrades will add to the cost (see our breakdown in [Custom Window Shade Costs](/blog/custom-window-shade-costs)).

## What Blackout Shades Actually Do

A blackout shade uses a fabric that has been either woven densely enough to admit zero light, or bonded with a light-blocking backing — usually a foam or acrylic coating applied to the fabric's reverse side. True blackout fabric achieves 100% opacity when the shade is fully deployed.

The catch is that fabric opacity is only half the equation. Light gaps around the edges, at the top rail, and at the bottom hem can still let in ambient glow even when the fabric itself is perfectly opaque. This is a real-world issue that trips up a lot of buyers — we cover the full picture in [Blackout Shades With No Light Gaps](/blog/blackout-shades-no-light-gaps), including the hardware choices (side channels, cassette headrails) that actually seal the window.

Blackout shades range from **$90 to $280 per window** for custom sizes. The higher end of that range typically includes cassette headrails and side channels that eliminate light gaps — hardware that's well worth the investment in a bedroom or media room.

Where blackout shades excel:

- **Bedrooms** — especially for shift workers, infants, or anyone sensitive to early morning light
- **Home theaters and media rooms** — glare on a TV or projector screen is immediately eliminated
- **Guest rooms** — gives guests control over sleep without requiring blackout curtains layered on top
- **Nurseries** — consistent darkness supports infant and toddler sleep schedules

## Solar Shades vs. Blackout: The Core Tradeoffs

The decision between solar shades and blackout comes down to three variables: **light control**, **privacy**, and **view preservation**. No single shade type wins across all three.

| | Solar Shades | Blackout Shades |
|---|---|---|
| Daytime glare reduction | Excellent | Complete (but no view) |
| UV protection | 95–99% depending on openness | 100% |
| Daytime privacy | Partial (interior visible in bright rooms) | Complete |
| Nighttime privacy | Poor (interior visible when lit) | Complete |
| Outside view | Preserved (varies by openness %) | None |
| Natural light feel | Diffused, warm | None — room is dark |
| Energy efficiency | High (reflects solar heat) | High (insulates window) |

Both shade types contribute meaningfully to energy efficiency, just through different mechanisms. Solar shades reflect solar radiation before it enters the room, reducing cooling loads in summer. Blackout shades add an insulating layer that reduces both heat gain in summer and heat loss in winter. Either way, the window treatment is doing real thermal work — see [Energy-Efficient Window Shades](/blog/energy-efficient-window-shades) for the data on how much each type can reduce energy bills.

## Room-by-Room Recommendations

**Living Room and Family Room**

Solar shades are almost always the right call here. These are spaces you use during daylight hours, where you want natural light and outdoor views but not glare on the TV or screens. A 3% or 5% openness solar shade handles both goals well. If you have floor-to-ceiling windows or a strong west exposure, step down to 1% to tame afternoon sun.

**Primary Bedroom**

Blackout shades. Sleep quality is directly tied to light exposure, and even a modest amount of morning light can fragment sleep in people who are light-sensitive. If you also want the option of natural light during the day, a dual-roller system pairs a solar shade with a blackout shade on a single headrail — you get both. It's one of the most popular configurations we make at World Wide Shades.

**Home Office**

This depends on your monitor setup. If you face a window, a solar shade between 1% and 3% openness controls screen glare while keeping the room from feeling like a cave. If your monitor is positioned perpendicular to the window, a 5% openness shade is usually enough. Pure blackout shades are rarely needed in home offices unless you use a projector or work overnight.

**Kitchen and Dining**

Solar shades at 3%–5% openness work well. Kitchens benefit from natural light, and most people don't need full blackout in these spaces. For dining rooms with east or west exposures, 1%–3% openness handles direct sun during meal times without blocking the view entirely.

**Nursery and Kids' Rooms**

Blackout shades, full stop. Nap schedules and early-morning wake-ups are the primary issue, and nothing fixes both like true darkness. Pair with side channels and a cassette headrail for the most complete light seal.

**Basement and Media Room**

Blackout shades, and consider motorization so you can control them without walking across the room. We've covered the full case for motorized systems in [Motorized Roller Shades](/blog/motorized-roller-shades-worth-it) — in a media room, the convenience argument is hard to beat.

## Getting the Fit Right

Neither solar nor blackout shades perform at their best if they're poorly sized. A solar shade that's an inch too narrow develops a sidelighting problem; a blackout shade that's measured wrong lets in enough light to disrupt sleep.

For blackout shades specifically, outside-mount installation is usually preferable because the shade extends beyond the window frame, covering the frame edges that let light bleed through in an inside mount. The full breakdown of when each mounting method makes sense is in [Inside Mount vs. Outside Mount](/blog/inside-mount-vs-outside-mount-roller-shades), and step-by-step sizing instructions are in [How to Measure Windows](/blog/how-to-measure-windows-roller-shades).

For solar roller shades, inside mounts work well in most cases because the open-weave fabric isn't trying to seal the frame — it's filtering light across the glass area.

## Custom vs. Off-the-Shelf

You can find both solar and blackout shades at big-box retailers in standard sizes, typically 23", 27", 35", 47", or 59" widths. For windows that fall outside those increments — which is most windows in older homes, especially in New York City — you're either trimming fabric in ways that compromise edge finish, or living with gaps.

## The Dual-Roller Option

If you genuinely need both solar and blackout performance in the same room, a dual-roller shade is the most seamless solution. Two separate fabric rolls share a single headrail — typically with the solar shade at the front and the blackout shade at the rear. During the day you deploy the solar; at night or during a nap you lower the blackout. Each shade operates independently.

Dual-roller systems run **$180 to $400 per window** depending on fabric selections and motorization. For a bedroom that doubles as a reading room or home office, that investment pays off quickly in flexibility.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can solar shades provide privacy during the day?

During the day, tighter-weave solar shades (1%–3% openness) make it difficult to see into your home from outside because of the light differential — the exterior is brighter than the interior. However, this reverses at night. Once your interior lights are on after dark, most solar fabrics become translucent from the outside. If daytime and nighttime privacy both matter, pair a solar shade with a blackout shade in a dual-roller configuration.

### Do blackout shades completely block all light?

The fabric itself blocks 100% of light. The practical limitation is light gaps at the edges of the window frame, at the top rail, and at the bottom hem. To eliminate those, you need a cassette headrail (which covers the rolled fabric and seals the top), side channels (which guide the fabric edge against the frame), and a proper bottom-rail fit. See [Blackout Shades With No Light Gaps](/blog/blackout-shades-no-light-gaps) for the full rundown on hardware options.

### What openness percentage should I choose for a solar shade?

For most living rooms and home offices, 3% openness is the right starting point — it reduces glare significantly, blocks about 97% of UV rays, and still gives you a clear view outside. If you have an intense south- or west-facing exposure, drop to 1%. If your room is north-facing or you want maximum brightness, 5% or 10% openness keeps the room feeling open. We recommend ordering free swatches so you can hold the fabric up to your actual window before deciding.

### Are solar shades worth it for energy savings?

Yes, measurably. Solar shades reflect solar radiation before it enters the room, which reduces cooling loads during summer months. Studies on solar control window films (which operate on the same principle as solar shade fabrics) show cooling cost reductions of 10%–25% in sun-intensive climates. In New York City specifically, south- and west-facing windows benefit the most. The savings are proportional to how much direct sun the window receives — north-facing windows get less benefit. Full data and context in [Energy-Efficient Window Shades](/blog/energy-efficient-window-shades).
