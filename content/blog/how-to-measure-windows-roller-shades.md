Measuring is the single most important step when ordering custom roller shades. Get it right and your shades fit perfectly — clean edges, smooth operation, no gaps. Get it wrong and you're reordering, paying shipping twice, and waiting all over again. The good news: with the right tools and a clear process, measuring your windows takes about ten minutes and requires zero prior experience. At World Wide Shades, we've processed tens of thousands of custom orders and the number-one cause of fit problems is a measurement taken incorrectly — this guide will make sure that doesn't happen to you.

## Tools You'll Need

Before you touch a window, gather these four things:

- **Steel tape measure** — Not a cloth sewing tape, not a flexible plastic one. Cloth tapes stretch; flexible tapes bow in the middle. A 1/8″ error on a custom roller shade means visible gaps or a shade that won't fit.
- **Pencil** — So you can correct mistakes without confusion.
- **Notepad or phone** — You will take three measurements per dimension, per window. Write every number down immediately.
- **Step stool** — For high windows, a stable step stool ensures you're measuring straight across, not at an angle. Angled measurements read longer than the actual opening.

---

## Step 1 — Decide: Inside or Outside Mount

Before you measure anything, you need to know where the shade will be mounted, because the two methods require completely different measurements.

- **Inside mount**: The shade installs inside the window frame, sitting within the casing. It gives a clean, built-in look and requires at least 1.5″ of frame depth for most roller shades.
- **Outside mount**: The shade installs on the wall or molding above the window, covering the frame entirely. Best if your frame is shallow, has obstructions (alarm sensors, window cranks), or if you want maximum light blockage.

For a full breakdown of the pros, cons, and visual differences, see our guide on [inside vs. outside mount roller shades](/blog/inside-mount-vs-outside-mount-roller-shades). Once you know which mount you're using, continue below.

## Step 2 — Measuring for Inside Mount

Windows are almost never perfectly square, which is exactly why you measure in multiple spots.

**Measure the width** at three points — top, middle, and bottom of the opening — edge-to-edge of the window frame. Write down all three numbers. **Use the narrowest measurement.** This ensures the shade won't bind against the frame at its tightest point.

**Measure the height** at three points — left, center, and right — from the top of the opening down to the windowsill. **Use the longest measurement.** This ensures full coverage at the tallest point of the opening.

Round all measurements to the nearest 1/8″.

**Do NOT make your own deductions.** This is critical. Manufacturers automatically deduct for hardware clearance — typically 1/8″ to 1/4″ off the width — so the shade moves smoothly. If you pre-deduct and the factory also deducts, your shade will be too small. Submit your exact opening measurements and let the factory handle fit.

**Check frame depth.** Before finalizing inside mount, measure from the face of the frame back toward the glass at three points. Most roller shades need a minimum of **1.5″ of depth** for brackets. If you're under that, switch to outside mount.

---

## Step 3 — Measuring for Outside Mount

Outside mount gives you more control — you're deciding the coverage, not measuring a fixed opening.

**Width**: Start with the window frame width, then add overlap on each side to block light and create a proportional look. The standard recommendation is **2–3″ of overlap per side** (4–6″ total). Measure from your intended left edge to your intended right edge. Outside mount shades are made to your exact specification.

**Height**: Choose your mount point. Mounting 2–3″ above the window molding keeps the roller mechanism out of the glass line when the shade is raised and makes the window appear taller. Mark the spot, then measure straight down to wherever the shade should end — typically the sill or 2–3″ below it for additional coverage.

## The 5 Most Common Measuring Mistakes

1. **Using a cloth or flexible tape measure.** These stretch and sag, introducing errors of 1/4″ or more. Use a rigid steel tape measure, every time.

2. **Only measuring in one spot.** A window that reads 36″ at the top may be 35 3/4″ in the middle. Measure three times per dimension and use the controlling number (narrowest width, longest height).

3. **Making your own deductions for inside mount.** The factory deducts based on your specific hardware. If you deduct too, the shade arrives undersized. Don't second-guess the process.

4. **Forgetting to check frame depth.** If you skip this and order inside mount for a window with 1″ of depth, the brackets won't have anything to grip. Always verify minimum depth before committing to inside mount.

5. **Measuring the glass instead of the full opening.** You need edge-to-edge of the window frame, not the glass pane. The glass sits recessed within the frame — measuring it will leave you short by an inch or more on each dimension.

## Special Cases

**Bay windows**: Measure each panel individually — never wall to wall. Even panels that look identical often differ by 1/8″ to 1/4″. Label each measurement by position (left, center, right) and order each shade separately.

**Corner windows**: The brackets of adjacent shades can physically interfere with each other. Before measuring, check the clearance at the corner. For outside mounts on corner windows, confirm at least 1.5″–2″ of flat wall surface is available at each corner edge for bracket placement.

**Recessed windows**: Deep recesses in older brick or stone homes can vary significantly in depth from point to point. Measure depth at multiple locations — corners and center — and use the shallowest reading. A shade that fits the opening width may still bind as it travels if the recess narrows toward the glass.

**Sliding glass doors**: Always use outside mount for sliding glass doors. The frames are typically too shallow for inside mount, and outside mount gives you the coverage needed to block light on a large opening. For complete sliding door measuring guidance, see our [sliding glass door window treatment guide](/blog/window-shades-sliding-glass-doors).

**Large windows over 72″ wide**: Errors compound on wide windows. A 1/4″ mistake becomes a visible gap. For these windows, consider professional measuring — the cost is almost always less than the cost of one reorder. For more on large-window shade engineering, see our [custom roller shades for large windows guide](/blog/custom-roller-shades-large-windows).

## When to Call a Professional

Most standard windows are easy to measure on your own. Three situations where professional measuring pays off:

- **Very large windows** (over 72″ wide or 84″ tall), where a small error becomes a significant problem
- **Old homes with out-of-square frames** — if your diagonal measurements (corner to corner) differ by more than 1/2″, the window is significantly racked and needs a professional assessment
- **Multi-window orders** where visual consistency matters — for 8, 10, or 15 shades in the same room, even small inconsistencies in measuring create visible differences in hang height and fabric exposure

The cost of professional measuring is almost always less than the cost of one reorder.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**My width came out to 35 9/16″. What do I order?**
Round down to the nearest 1/8″. Since 9/16″ falls between 4/8″ and 5/8″, round down to 35.5″. For width, always round down. For height, always round up. At World Wide Shades, we accept measurements to the nearest 1/8" and our team is available to review your numbers before you finalize an order.

**Do I need to measure the same window twice?**
Yes. Measure each dimension three times (top, middle, bottom for width; left, center, right for height) and record all numbers. Never assume a single measurement is accurate.

**Can I use a laser measuring tool?**
You can, but verify with a steel tape until you're confident in the device. Laser tools can be thrown off by reflective glass or oblique angles. When in doubt, use both and compare.

**I have a window crank inside the frame. Can I still do inside mount?**
It depends on whether the crank protrudes into the shade's travel path. If the hardware isn't deep enough to clear the crank, outside mount is the right call — no workarounds needed.
