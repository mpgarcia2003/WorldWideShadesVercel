- Typical total per window (shade + motor): $300–$1,200

## What drives the price up the most
### 1) Size: width and drop
A small bathroom window and a 96-inch-wide slider are not the same product.
As widths climb past about 72 inches, manufacturers often increase tube diameter and use heavier brackets.

### 2) Fabric performance level
Even within the same category, fabrics vary by opacity, weave density, and coatings.
For solar and light-filtering options, compare fabrics in [Light Filtering Shades Guide](/blog/how-to-install-roller-shades).

### 3) Hardware upgrades
Better clutches, metal chains, and upgraded brackets increase durability.
If you’re comparing DIY big-box options, see [Custom Roller Shades vs Home Depot](/blog/custom-roller-shades-vs-home-depot).

### 4) Installation complexity
Odd surfaces, tall ladders, and multi-window alignment add labor.
A typical professional install runs $50–$150 per window depending on access.

## Motorized roller shade pricing by control type
### Remote control (single room)
Remote systems are typically the most cost-effective motorized option.

Multi-channel remotes and wall switches add convenience and can reduce clutter.

### Smart home integration (Alexa / Google / app)

## Battery vs hardwired: how it changes cost
### Battery motorization
- Usually faster and cheaper to install.
- Great for retrofit projects where opening drywall is not desirable.
- Budget: often $300–$1,000 per window depending on fabric and size.

### Plug-in (low-voltage) motorization
- Often used when a nearby outlet exists.
- The motor cost can be similar to battery, but cord routing can add labor.

### Hardwired motorization
- Best for new construction or full remodels.
- Highest labor cost because wiring is involved.
- Many homeowners budget $150–$400 per window for electrical work when wiring is not already in place.

## Cost checklist before you order
### Confirm your window count and grouping
If you have 10+ windows, grouping decisions matter: you may prefer one remote per room, or one remote for the whole floor.
Planning this upfront can avoid buying extra hubs or remotes later.

### Decide your daily-use schedule
If you plan to open/close shades twice daily, prioritize reliable motors and simple controls.
If you only adjust occasionally, you may be fine with fewer smart features.

### Measure for consistent alignment
Motorized shades look best when hem lines align across adjacent windows.
Even a 1/2-inch difference in mounting height can be noticeable on a big wall of glass.
