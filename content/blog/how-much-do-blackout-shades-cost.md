## What drives the price up the most
### 1) Size: width and drop
A small bathroom window and a 96-inch-wide slider are not the same product.
As widths climb past about 72 inches, manufacturers often increase tube diameter and use heavier brackets.

### 2) Fabric performance level
Even within the same category, fabrics vary by opacity, weave density, and coatings.

### 3) Hardware upgrades
Better clutches, metal chains, and upgraded brackets increase durability.

### 4) Installation complexity
Odd surfaces, tall ladders, and multi-window alignment add labor.
A typical professional install runs $50–$150 per window depending on access.

## Blackout shade costs by performance level
### “Room-darkening” (good for most bedrooms)
Room-darkening fabrics reduce most light but may leave edge glow.
Budget roughly $150–$450 per window for common sizes.

### True blackout (best for nurseries and very light sleepers)
True blackout fabrics block light through the fabric itself.
Budget roughly $200–$600 per window depending on size and hardware.

### Near-total blackout with side channels
Side channels reduce side gaps and are the most reliable approach.
Budget roughly $280–$850 per window when channels are added.

## What makes blackout shades expensive (and what doesn’t)
### Fabric opacity is only part of the story
A blackout fabric can still leak light around the edges.
Hardware choices—outside mount, returns, and side channels—often matter more than people expect.

### Wider windows increase hardware requirements
As widths grow, stronger tubes reduce deflection and help the shade track cleanly.
That can increase cost even before you add side channels.

### The “blackout tax” is usually modest
In many product lines, upgrading from light-filtering to blackout is often a $30–$120 per window difference.
That’s why it’s smart to choose blackout in bedrooms upfront if you think you’ll want it later.

## How to reduce light gaps (practical numbers)
- Inside mount: expect small side gaps because the shade must clear the frame.
- Outside mount: you can “overlap” the window by about 1–3 inches per side to reduce edge light.
- Side channels: can reduce edge light dramatically, but require a cleaner mounting surface.

## More FAQs
### Are blackout shades the same as blackout curtains?
Not exactly.
Curtains can be more forgiving for odd shapes, but shades can look cleaner and take up less space.

### Should I choose a cassette for blackout?
A cassette can reduce top glow, but side gaps usually matter more.
If you want the cleanest finish, combine a cassette with an outside mount and consider channels.

They can reduce radiant heat and drafts near windows, especially when paired with energy-aware fabrics.

## Blackout cost planning for common window sizes
### Standard bedroom window (rough planning numbers)
For many standard bedroom windows, homeowners commonly land in the $200–$500 range for blackout when choosing quality fabric and clean hardware.
The same room in light-filtering may be closer to $160–$420, depending on your selections.

### Large windows and sliders
For wide windows, blackout can increase costs because heavier tubes help the fabric track smoothly.
If you’re covering a wide opening, consider whether you want one wide shade or multiple narrower shades for easier handling.

## Blackout vs room-darkening: how to pick without regret
