Arched windows are one of the most striking architectural features a home can have. They draw the eye, flood rooms with natural light, and lend an unmistakable sense of elegance to any space — until you need to cover them. The moment you measure for window treatments, the problem becomes clear: standard shades are built for rectangles. Arched windows are not rectangles. The result is bare arches, mismatched patchwork solutions, or costly mistakes from products that simply were not designed for curved openings. At World Wide Shades, we've covered more arched windows than we can count — and we've learned that the right solution depends heavily on the specific arch type and how the window is used.

This guide covers every viable option for custom shades for arched windows, what each one costs, how to measure correctly, and how to avoid the most common ordering mistakes.

## Why Standard Shades Can't Fit Arched Windows

Standard window shades, blinds, and shutters are manufactured with straight bottom rails and vertical side channels designed for 90-degree corners. They work perfectly on rectangular openings and nothing else.

Arched windows introduce two fundamental problems:

**Curved edges.** A standard shade's straight fabric edge cannot follow the curve of an arch. Installing one creates triangular gaps of light on each side — defeating the purpose of any shade entirely.

**Mounting incompatibility.** Standard headrail brackets mount at the sides of a rectangular frame and rely on vertical side channels for guidance. An arch has no vertical sides at the top — the frame curves inward. Standard hardware has no way to conform to or anchor against a radius.

The solution is custom fabrication. Every option below is built to match your window's exact curve, which is why ordering precise measurements (or a paper template) is non-negotiable. Before you finalize measurements, see our [complete guide to measuring windows for roller shades](/blog/how-to-measure-windows-roller-shades) for the underlying principles that apply to arched windows as well.

## Your 4 Best Options for Arched Window Coverings

### 1. Cellular Arch Shades

Cellular arch shades — also called honeycomb arch shades — are the most popular solution for arched windows, and for good reason. The pleated honeycomb fabric fans out naturally from a central mounting point at the top of the arch, following the curve down to the base without any cutting or forcing. The result looks clean and built-in, as though the shade was always part of the window.

**Stationary vs. operable.** Most cellular arch shades are stationary — they are installed once and stay in place. This works well for arched windows that sit high on the wall or above a doorway where daily adjustment is impractical. However, operable cellular arch shades do exist for homeowners who want active light control. Hunter Douglas's Duette® Honeycomb Shades, for example, can be configured for specialty arch shapes with PowerView® Automation, allowing motorized operation via remote, app, or voice assistant. Blindsgalore offers the Bali Perfect Arch Cellular Shades in both light-filtering (3/4″ single-cell construction) and blackout (1/2″ double-cell construction) for half-circle and fan-shaped windows.

**Best insulation of any arch option.** The honeycomb cell structure traps air between the window glass and the room, creating a genuine thermal barrier. For large or high-set arched windows — common in great rooms, entryways, and stairwells — this is a meaningful energy benefit.

**Best for:** Homeowners who want the most popular, versatile, and energy-efficient solution. Works in virtually any interior style from transitional to modern to traditional.

### 2. Custom Shutters

Custom shutters are the premium tier of arched window treatments, offering the most dramatic and architecturally considered look of any option. Unlike shades, which cover the arch, shutters become part of the window's design.

Two configurations are available:

- **Sunburst arch shutters** feature louvers that radiate outward from the center in a fan pattern, echoing the arch's geometry and creating an unmistakable visual statement. These are typically fixed-louver installations.
- **Continuous arch shutters** use a fully framed panel system that follows the curve of the opening with adjustable louvers throughout. These are handcrafted and represent the highest level of customization.

A third hybrid approach — popular for cost savings — pairs standard rectangular plantation shutters on the lower portion of a window with a fixed sunburst arch panel above. The sunburst matches the curve while the lower shutters provide functional light control.

Shutters are especially well-suited for traditional, Mediterranean, colonial, and coastal-style homes where the architectural character warrants a substantial treatment. Material choices include real wood ($200–$600 per window base) and composite/faux wood ($100–$400 per window base), with arch customization adding 15–25% to those figures.

**Best for:** Homeowners prioritizing aesthetics and long-term value. Shutters carry a 50+ year lifespan and are widely recognized as a home value enhancer.

### 3. Motorized Roller Shades With Outside Mount

For homeowners who want complete light blocking on demand — without any permanent shade visible in the arch — a motorized roller shade with an outside mount is the most functional solution.

The mechanics are straightforward: the roller shade mounts above the arch on the wall or ceiling, completely outside the window opening. When the shade is lowered, it covers the entire window — arch, rectangular base, and all — in one uninterrupted panel. When fully raised, the shade retracts into the headrail above the arch, revealing the complete architectural form with nothing obstructing the view.

This approach requires a motorized lift system because the headrail is mounted high enough that manual operation would require a ladder. Motorized options from manufacturers like Hunter Douglas (PowerView® Automation) and Somfy-compatible systems make this practical and seamless, with control via remote, smartphone app, or smart home integration. For a full breakdown of motorized shade costs and smart home compatibility, see our [motorized roller shades guide](/blog/motorized-roller-shades-worth-it).

**Best for:** Homeowners who want full blackout capability, clean aesthetics when the shade is raised, and don't want a permanent shade visible in the arch. Ideal for home theaters, bedrooms, and media rooms with dramatic arched windows.

### 4. Fixed Fabric Shades

Fixed fabric shades are the most budget-conscious arched window treatment. A piece of custom-cut fabric — typically light-filtering or room-darkening — is shaped to match the arch and installed as a stationary insert, similar in concept to a stationary cellular shade but at a lower price point.

The primary design application is coordination: a fixed fabric arch shade, matched in color, opacity, and texture to operating cellular or roller shades on adjacent or lower rectangular windows, creates a unified and polished look at a fraction of the cost of shutters or motorized systems. They provide consistent light diffusion and privacy without any moving parts.

**Best for:** Secondary rooms, rental properties, or any project where budget is the primary concern and daily light adjustment is not needed.

## Perfect Arch vs. Non-Perfect Arch — Why It Matters

Before you order any arched window treatment, you need to know which type of arch you have. This single determination affects the ordering process, the template requirements, and the final cost.

**A perfect arch** is a true semicircle: the height of the curved portion is exactly half of the window's width. A 48″ wide window with a 24″ tall arch is a perfect arch. The curve is consistent at every point. Most cellular arch shades and shutters can be ordered online for perfect arches using just two measurements: width at the widest point and height from base to the top of the curve.

**A non-perfect arch** is anything else — an arch where the height is not exactly half the width, where the curve is asymmetrical, or where the radius varies at different points along the arc. Even a deviation of 1/16″ changes the shape enough to affect fit. Non-perfect arches require a paper template. To create one, tape paper over the window opening, trace the exact curve, and cut along the line. Manufacturers like Blindsgalore walk customers through this process with their design team before fabrication begins.

Non-perfect arches take longer to fabricate and typically cost more due to the additional customization required.

## Palladian Windows: Treating the Arch and Rectangle Together

A Palladian window — an arched top combined with a rectangular window below, sometimes flanked by narrower side windows — is one of the most common places arched window treatments appear. The challenge is achieving a unified look across two fundamentally different shapes.

The most effective approach is the **arch-over-standard** configuration. For windows under 40″ wide, most manufacturers (including Blindsgalore's Select and Bali product lines) offer a combined system: a stationary cellular arch shade on top connects via a slotted headrail to an operable cellular shade below. The arch shade slots directly into the top of the lower shade's headrail, creating one seamless, integrated treatment. Match fabric color, cell size, and opacity level between both sections for a look that reads as intentional rather than assembled.

For wider Palladian windows, a **Palladian shelf** can be installed at the point where the arch begins, visually and functionally separating the two sections and allowing each to be treated independently. Blindsgalore offers a dedicated Palladian Shelf accessory for this purpose, available in multiple finish colors.

## How to Measure an Arched Window

**For a perfect arch (online ordering):**
1. Measure the **width** at the widest point of the opening — this is the base of the arch.
2. Measure the **height** from the base of the arch to the highest point of the curve.
3. Verify that the height is exactly half the width before ordering. A 36″ wide window should have an 18″ arch height to qualify as a perfect arch.
4. Measure at multiple points along the curve to confirm consistency.

**For a non-perfect arch (template required):**
1. Tape craft paper or contractor paper over the entire arch opening.
2. Trace the exact contour of the frame opening onto the paper from behind.
3. Cut along the traced line and verify the template lies flat against the window.
4. Mail or photograph the template and contact the manufacturer's design team before placing an order.

For outside-mount motorized roller shades, measure the **total area you want to cover**, including several inches of overlap on each side and above the arch to ensure full light block. ## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use regular blinds on an arched window?**
Standard horizontal blinds cannot bend to follow a curve, so they cannot cover an arched opening. They can cover the rectangular portion of a Palladian window, but the arch itself requires a treatment specifically designed for curved shapes — such as a cellular arch shade or custom shutter.

**Are arched window shades always stationary?**
Most cellular arch shades are stationary, but operable options exist. Hunter Douglas's Duette® Honeycomb Shades with PowerView® Automation can be motorized for arched and angled shapes. Outside-mount roller shades are always fully operable, raising and lowering to cover or reveal the entire window.

**What is the difference between a perfect and non-perfect arch, and does it affect price?**
A perfect arch has a height exactly equal to half its width and a consistent radius throughout. A non-perfect arch deviates from that ratio or has an irregular curve. Non-perfect arches require a custom paper template before ordering and typically cost 15–25% more due to the additional fabrication complexity. At World Wide Shades, we fabricate both perfect and non-perfect arch shades — for non-standard arches, we'll guide you through the template process to ensure a precise fit.

**How do I treat a Palladian window where the arch sits above a regular window?**
The standard approach is an arch-over-standard cellular shade system — a stationary arch shade on top connects via a slotted headrail to an operable shade below. Match fabric color, opacity, and cell construction between the two sections for a cohesive look. For windows wider than 40″, a Palladian shelf that separates the arch from the rectangular section is often recommended.
