The most common question we get from customers: "Should I go blackout or light filtering?" The answer depends entirely on the room. Here's our room-by-room guide.

## Understanding the Difference

**Blackout shades** block 99%+ of incoming light. When fully lowered, the room goes dark. The fabric has an opaque backing that prevents any light transmission. Ideal for sleeping, media rooms, and rooms with screen glare.

**Light filtering shades** softly diffuse incoming sunlight while maintaining privacy. You get a warm, glowing ambient light without the harsh direct rays. Think of it like frosted glass — you can tell it's bright outside, but you can't see details or get direct sun.

## Room-by-Room Recommendations

### Bedrooms — Blackout
This one's straightforward. Bedrooms need darkness for quality sleep. If you're a light sleeper, work night shifts, or have kids who nap during the day, blackout is non-negotiable. The investment in better sleep pays dividends in every other area of life.

### Living Room — Light Filtering
Living rooms benefit from natural light. Light filtering shades let you enjoy the warmth and brightness of daylight while reducing glare on TV screens and protecting furniture from UV damage. They also maintain privacy from street-level views during the day.

### Home Office — Light Filtering (or Dual)
For home offices, light filtering shades reduce screen glare while maintaining pleasant ambient light — important for video calls and reducing eye strain. If your office doubles as a guest bedroom, consider motorized dual shades that offer both options in one window.

### Nursery — Blackout
As we covered in our [nursery guide](/blog/blackout-shades-nursery-guide), babies need complete darkness for optimal sleep. Always go blackout for nurseries and children's rooms.

### Kitchen — Light Filtering
Kitchens are workspaces that benefit from natural light. Light filtering shades brighten the room while protecting against direct sun. They're also easier to keep clean in splash-prone areas since you won't notice minor spots as easily.

### Bathroom — Light Filtering
Privacy is the primary concern in bathrooms. Light filtering shades provide excellent privacy while still letting in soft natural light. For bathrooms with tubs, look for moisture-resistant fabrics.

### Media Room — Blackout
Any room with a projector or large screen TV benefits from blackout shades. Even light filtering shades let in enough ambient light to wash out projected images.
